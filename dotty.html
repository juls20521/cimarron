Description

Dorothy rarely wears a dress. She prefers a simple shirt and man's trousers that she really does take some effort to keep clean, but inevitably it has a dirt mark or stain upon her outfit somewhere soon enough. She doesn't sport much jewelry. Just a heavy belt buckle and a single chain with two mismatched charms: a small copper pendant with two tiny emeralds embedded into it and a rattlesnake tail stuck in spent casing. This she usually keeps tucked beneath a scarf or under her shirt.

She wears too much eye makeup, darkening her brown eyes. Heavy rouge colors her freckled cheeks and pale complexion. Her lips she never paints. Even when they start to get a bit chapped in the winter, she dislikes having anything on them. Her blonde hair, well overdue for a trim, is often pulled into a pony tail or a braid that starts off neat but usually ends up falling loose beneath her brown and black hat that she wears like a shield, using it to hide her expression and emotions when her 'tough girl' persona starts to slip.

If she'll let you get close enough, she often smells of horses and whiskey with a small hint of mint. Around her hips she wears a pistol belt that contains her matching set of Smith & Wesson Schofield Revolvers.

Biography

Born in the spring of 1846 in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania. Though her exact birthdate is unknown, it was shortly after the time of the Great Fire that destroyed nearly a third of the city, leaving her parents struggling to rebuild their lives after losing everything to the flames. For a few years, they did. Her father worked in the coal mines to support his wife and 3 children. Dorothy was the youngest. James the oldest (born in '37), then Emma (born in '39).

Their father died in '51 from a mining accident and things declined from there when their mother had trouble coping both financially and emotionally, turning to alcohol way too often, leaving Dorothy's upbringing mostly to her two older siblings. With little guidance, the young girl found herself left to her own devices more often than not, which led to minor trouble and petty thievery. After their mother's death in 1863, James, tired of the coal mines and anxious to avoid military service, decided to venture west. Dorothy, barely 17 at the time, chose to go with him.

Darker Times: The brother and sister spent the next six months traveling slowly west, both picking up odd jobs. When funds were low, they'd sometimes resort to less than scrupulous ways of making money. Which usually meant Dorothy offering her "services" for the night and then she and James robbing him of his valuables. This caught up to them in Texas when they unknowingly intruded upon the territory of Owen Donley, who ran several established brothels in the area. When he caught wind of this, he sent several men to put a stop to it. Which meant a bullet for James. And Dorothy was immediately added to his collection of whores, where she spent the next 3 years.

Hell to Pay: In '66, the notorious outlaw Robert Baker (a.k.a. "One-Eyed Bobby") and his crew stopped for a night of fun at Donley's brothel. Bobby took a shine to Dorothy and, despite a bit of protesting from Owen Donley, took her with him. For nearly three years, she warmed his bedroll. For the most part, he kept her out of his criminal endeavors. But anxious to prove her worth, she convinced him to let her participate in a train robbery in 1869. The robbery was a success. The Baker Gang stole a safe carrying thousands in Wells Fargo money and got away clean. But in the following weeks it all came crashing down. Betrayed by two of their own, the Baker Gang walked right into an ambush. Robert Baker and Sparrow Hawk were both shot dead while Freddy Linch, Paul Smith, and Dorothy fled north to Wyoming.
